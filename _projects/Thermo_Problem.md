## Overview

The plant uses two Ossberger cross-flow turbines. We chose to analyze the second unit listed on the Cornell Facilities page, named the **Crossflow G1078/20g (2 cell)**.

In these types of systems, water typically flows in from a body of water and passes through inlet guides. These regulate flow rate and direct the water across the turbineâ€™s blades twice, hence the **cross-flow** name, maximizing the amount of energy that can be harnessed. Water finally exits on the opposite side and continues through the waterway.

<img src="{{ '/assets/images/thermo-ec/ossberger_diagram.png' | relative_url }}" alt="Ossberger Diagram" style="width:100%; display:block;">
<p><strong>Source:</strong> Ossberger</p>

<img src="{{ '/assets/images/thermo-ec/flow_diagram.png' | relative_url }}" alt="Flow Diagram" style="width:100%; display:block;">
<p><strong>Source:</strong> Pump Fundamentals</p>

A benefit of cross-flow turbines is that they operate adequately at a variety of flow rates, and their efficiency remains relatively constant over a wide range of inputs. This is ideal for small river applications like this one, where flow is far from constant under the variable weather conditions of Ithaca.
